Not sure if any of you saw the following post from Ryan (Atlasquest owner, webmaster and moderator)  on the Atlasquest New York board, but here it is, along with a personal note I received from Ryan and my posted response. Can we have a discussion about this? I would love to hear points of view from others and how they handled their similar  situation. Did you encounter nice Park personnel willing to work with you? Did you design learning  about the location into your stamp/box?  Did you just pull the box or did you ask for a permit? Let's hear from you all out there!

Post from Ryan on Atlasquest: Hello, New Yorkers! =) I got this e-mail a few minutes ago from a guy in the New York SP system:

"You should be advised that permission is needed via permit from New York State Park Managers in order to place a letter box in a state park. There are rules for geocaches that also apply to letterboxes. (state park management has set spefic rules for placement of such things) Most park managers will work with you in placement of the letterboxes when done so through the park manager. When letterboxes are found without the proper permit they will most likely be removed and the owner notified."

I'm not sure if he's refering to a specific box or not, but let it be known, permits are needed if you want to plant a box in a New York SP and they DO know about Atlas Quest so don't think you can sneak around them by posting here instead of LbNA. ;o)


Mail from Ryan:

I wasn't sure if the ranger from the New York State Parks was e-mailing me about a specific box or not and asked, and this was the reply:

"Ryan,
I apologize for not being specific. There was a box put in the Minna Anthony
Common Nature Center called something like Girls Night Out, I found it, removed itand emailed the owner. Getting permission to put the box in a park is quite easy
the person just has to ask and follow the rules! Thanks for getting back to me.

Randy "

Is that you? =) I can't find a box called "Girls Night Out" on AQ, but I swear I've heard it before and I swear it sounds like something you would do. ;o)

-- Ryan

My response on AQ

Ryan -

Thanks for posting about this. I am not that thrilled about planting in state parks anyway, as I try to bring people to those lesser known places that they might not have seen before. Most of my 16 (out of 215!) state park boxes are in lesser known and visited state parks. But having worked with NY State Parks personnel before (I used to work for a prominent architecture/landscape architecture firm here in NY), I tried to anticipate their concerns and so planted "location" based stamps. You know what I mean... the stamp was about the place and I gave a lot of background about the area and sang it's praises....trying to educate the public. I enjoy finding location stamps myself (so looking at my logbook is sort of like a roadmap of everywhere I've been!). I also love learning about new places and I find that searchers of my boxes are appreciative of that sort of thing as well. In the beginning, I just carved anything that struck my fancy, but now I carve mostly location boxes with perhaps the occasional nearby fun "bonus" box thrown in.

The few park personnel that HAVE contacted me have been really cool and happy to let me leave my boxes as is. Only one had my box moved slightly, as they were doing some trail work. When I plant, I make sure I avoid nesting areas and sensitive plants such as fern beds and the like. It's easy to pick up a brochure about the area BEFORE you plant, learn something about the place and then avoid the sensitive spots. I avoid animal holes in trees, placing boxes in ancient rock walls etc. I anticipate the path that might form to the box and so make that path over rocks or pine needles or similar non-sensitive ground cover. I learned from Catbead that under a bridge is a good hiding place for just this reason! All those long hikes with my parents while my mother droned on about plants and their latin names (blah blah blah) must have sunk in a little! lol

After I read your post, I went back through my list and was pleasantly surprised that my 16 state park boxes are in 10 parks. I think I will take the tactic of contacting each of the park personnel that I haven't spoken to already and let them know about my box, invite them to find it by providing them the clues, and asking them to contact me to pick up the box if they feel it is in an inappropriate area or needs a permit. I've introduced more than a couple of park personnel to letterboxing this way and they seem to enjoy the fact that I lead them RIGHT TO THE BOX, as the majority of my boxes are hidden with families in mind. I encourage all New Yorkers to give letterboxers a good reputation as land and nature stewards by doing the same.

LW

Although I love the secretive nature of this hobby, I do think that New York State Parks have done a good job of working WITH letterboxers, instead of banning the hobby as other states have done.  Please share your own experiences.

Geocachers have a written and posted policy in place to work with state park personnel BEFORE they plant a geocache. I am not saying that all geocachers follow this policy, but in general, the geocachers that I know ( do y'all know I was once a geocacher?... I gave it up pretty quickly) look upon letterboxers as more "rogue" players, in that the general consensus has been to "plant first and explain later." I am not saying all letterboxers do not ask permission before planting! This is only the impression I get from reading 15 different boards on this topic and speaking with over 100 experienced and inexperienced  letterboxers. Several state parks have been working closely with geocachers and have good relationships with them, and host many geocache events.such as those at Letchworth. Rather than bemoan the way  geocaching/geocachers operate, we can emulate one of the better ideas (respecting the fact that SP will now want permits for letterboxes) and either decide to remove our boxes and plant elsewhere or work with the system in place. I think it is a personal decision each letterboxer will have to make (Move the box? or  Register the box?)  but I am hoping that letterboxers will not try to circumvent the system and get all of our boxes pulled. Just my own opinion.

From: Tracy Baxter Haynes | Date: 2006-08-02 15:57:37 UTC
I have a series placed in a state park.  These boxes are singular to their location as they commemorate a yearly event at the park.  However, of the two personal decisions you propose, ('Move the box? or Register the box?') I will do neither.  I am definitely in the 'plant first, explain later' group of letterboxers.  Part of this is because of the inherent secrecy of the hobby.  While I know not everyone subscribes to this view of the hobby, and I certainly don't believe letterboxing should be 100% exclusionary, I do believe that secrecy or stealth is integral to the hobby.  If not, why do we *hide* boxes?  Why do you need clues to find boxes?  Why do we frown upon folks who give hints about box locations or publish stamp images?  Why do we use pseudonyms, i.e. trail names?  Why do we emphasize correct rehiding to keep the box from being found?  To me, part of this game's uniqueness lies in its 'under the radar' status and the stealth required to find the boxes undetected.
 
If we start asking permission, where do we stop?  All land is owned by someone - private parties or municipal authorities.  You can say we should ask permission of the state parks because they are the ones making an issue of it at this time.  But do we really think that town, city authorities, business owners or private property owners would really want boxes planted on their property if they knew of them?  If we believe it is the right of state park officials to regulate the planting of letterboxes on state property because they know of the hobby, isn't it a little intellectually dishonest to deprive another individual of the right to regulate planting on their property just because they haven't been informed of the practice?  Are we prepared to ask permission for every box we plant?
 
If a box is correctly and securely hid, ideally it should be found by no one without a clue in hand.  If it isn't hid or rehid securely and is found by a nonboxer, it is litter, pure and simple.  I can't tell you the times I have found boxes that were hid in questionable locations that couldn't, no way, no how, contain them secretly.  Numerous times I have come across boxes that were visible in their hiding places, saved only from detection in that they were in remote locations.  We need to start hiding and rehiding our boxes better so that detection of them is not an issue.  If a box is correctly hid and is findable only by a letterboxer, looking for it, clues in hand, and the regulating authority of the property, be it individual or organizational, never knows the box is there, then my take on it is 'no harm, no foul'.  This is just my 2 cents and (as Trailhoundz likes to say) with NYS taxes, worth about a penny. :-)

I have a series placed in a state park. These boxes are singular to
their location as they commemorate a yearly event at the park.
However, of the two personal decisions you propose, ('Move the box?
or Register the box?') I will do neither. I am definitely in
the 'plant first, explain later' group of letterboxers. Part of
this is because of the inherent secrecy of the hobby. While I know
not everyone subscribes to this view of the hobby, and I certainly
don't believe letterboxing should be 100% exclusionary, I do believe
that secrecy or stealth is integral to the hobby. If not, why do we
*hide* boxes? Why do you need clues to find boxes? Why do we frown
upon folks who give hints about box locations or publish stamp
images? Why do we use pseudonyms, i.e. trail names? Why do we
emphasize correct rehiding to keep the box from being found? To me,
part of this game's uniqueness lies in its 'under the radar' status
and the stealth required to find the boxes undetected.

If we start asking permission, where do we stop? All land is owned
by someone - private parties or municipal authorities. You can say
we should ask permission of the state parks because they are the
ones making an issue of it at this time. But do we really think
that town, city authorities, business owners or private property
owners would really want boxes planted on their property if they
knew of them? If we believe it is the right of state park officials
to regulate the planting of letterboxes on state property because
they know of the hobby, isn't it a little intellectually dishonest
to deprive another individual of the right to regulate planting on
their property just because they haven't been informed of the
practice? Are we prepared to ask permission for every box we plant?

If a box is correctly and securely hid, ideally it should be found
by no one without a clue in hand. If it isn't hid or rehid securely
and is found by a nonboxer, it is litter, pure and simple. I can't
tell you the times I have found boxes that were hid in questionable
locations that couldn't, no way, no how, contain them secretly.
Numerous times I have come across boxes that were visible in their
hiding places, saved only from detection in that they were in remote
locations. We need to start hiding and rehiding our boxes better so
that detection of them is not an issue. If a box is correctly hid
and is findable only by a letterboxer, looking for it, clues in
hand, and the regulating authority of the property, be it individual
or organizational, never knows the box is there, then my take on it
is 'no harm, no foul'. This is just my 2 cents and (as Trailhoundz
likes to say) with NYS taxes, worth about a penny. :-)

My point is, no one has to plant in State Parks!  There are literally thousands of places one can plant without dealing with State Park regulations at all! I like bringing people to lesser known places...but this has included some of the State Parks that could really use the funding that greater patronage brings. When I plant in a park I am hoping the letterbox enjoys the area enough to come back with family and friends.I don't need the park to put a sign up or provide maps to boxes, etc etc.  I support the parks and the NY State park personnel as they do their best to make sure these properties can be enjoyed by all. I don't want these beautiful properties to ever be sold to private owners or entities where they are fenced off from the public. My taxes support them, and I deserve access to these properties but am willing to help them protect and preserve them by playing by the rules. Yes, I don't bring glass container to the beaches; yes I will  make my campsite quiet by 10 pm so others can sleep: yes I will carry out anything I have carried in; yes I will follow their rules for safe and responsible placement of my boxes in their parks or risk eviction. I agree it is horrible that it has come to this, but that's why responsible education about our hobby  is so important.
 
In my own opinion, when I learned the Girl Scouts were publishing and promoting letterboxing ( I achieved the rank of Cadet, back in the day, btw) I knew letterboxing would be forever changed. Not that I have anything against the Scouts, as I know leaders (such as my mother) work hard to teach respect and knowledge about nature and playing by the rules. But just by sheer numbers of Scouts across the nation and publicity in Family Fun,  Time Magazine, etc..the days of no public knowledge about letterboxing are over...kaput..finished. But that is not to say that we cannot shape letterboxing into what we want it to be! Geocachers have been extremely successful at this, like Jackbear has stated. Their educational emphasis and Cache In-Trash Out policies and willingness to work with park personnel has endeared them to some agencies, as they have demonstrated a willingness to follow the rules and be responsible for their caches, or face their removal. Do all Geocachers play by their own rules? No. Do all letterboxers play by our limited rules? No. It's why proper education is going to save this hobby from bad press. Secrets shared on this big of a scale rarely remain secret. :)
 
As a mother, I am happy to share a hobby that gets kids (and adults!)  off the couch, exercising and into the woods or to a local historical site where they can learn a little more about the world around them. My own parents did this without the fun of letterboxing and I often wish letterboxing had been around to add another dimension to our fun. I also think it's good to set an example for kids that rules are in place for a reason. Any letterbox of mine that is discovered is an opportunity to show how I will work with the finder and hopefully introduce them to letterboxing in an educational way.
 
Do I have secret, WOM boxes in edgy places?  Yes.. Do I whisper clues in children's ears or drop a cootie into the bag of an unsuspecting boxer? Yep. Hasn't killed the secret aspect of letterboxing for me at all. In fact, besides the 200 boxes I have posted publicly so people can enjoy them,  I swap clues with folks I know,  just as those earliest of the letterboxers did with each other....
 
But don't ask me to get  "official sponsors" such as Jeep to "promote" letterboxing. I am liking the growth just fine without benefit of sponsors....and you'll set me off for an even bigger tirade....*grin* I think that is the day letterboxing will die for me.....
 
LW * who has been asked to teach a beginning letterbox class by the authorities at a National Historic Site..who discovered her box, were pleased by the historical lesson included and now lovingly maintain the box for her*

Chipping in my 2¢...and glad to see this topic
finally being discussed (thanks Ryan), as the
cat's been out of the bag on this one for a good
year, year and a half.

I like planting in State Parks. Most of my boxes
are in large (State) parks. Most of my boxes are
part of a series that will take the seeker along
beautiful trails, to explore and discover what
they might never see. This was my evil plan from
the beginning. :-) I love the particular parks
I've planted in, and if someone was willing to
come all this way to find my boxes, I wanted to
make it worth their while, especially after
paying an entrance fee. Now that the price of
gas is so high, I think it's even more important
that a boxer can drive to one spot, park and
spend the day boxing. I figure the entrance fee
is about the same as 2 gallons of gas, and for
those who have an Empire Pass like we have, then
by now it's already paid for itself and they get
in free.

When I started boxing there weren't many boxes in
this part of CNY, then a few years later it
really started to boom, and now they are
everywhere. :-) We've spent many a happy
afternoon doing these quick boxes, but now with
gas prices the way they are, not to mention the
exhaust/global warming thing (hello Toyota get
with the hybrid mini-van), I'm thinking twice
about spending $40 in gas on these local jaunts -
no matter how carefully mapped out they are it's
still a lot of starting, stopping, city traffic
driving (but I must say stamping in with the AC
blasting in a skeeter free atmosphere can't be
beat on a hot muggy).

So, the parks now require registration, and box
limits, and are finally turning their attention
to letterboxes. Well, that's the way it is.
That they are in friendly attitude to us is
because of geocachers who behaved responsibly is
to my (all of our) benefit. If a limit is
enforced, then for a series, I'll probably rotate
what's out, for example in an 8 part series have
the first 4 planted for so long then pull them
and plant the last 4. And so on.

Things change in life and one just has to roll with it.

I think the intention is that you list a box you don't plan to
replace as "retired," if you do plan to replace it it's "missing."
The thing that drives me nuts is that there doesn't seem to be a way
to label stuff that way on lbna. So when I look at my local boxes
there, there are a ton of boxes listed in Niagara county that aren't
really there because the folks who planted them pulled their boxes
when they moved out of the area. The planters (Team Dakota) were
responsible and changed the clues to say that the boxes were no
longer there but had been pulled, I just wish lbna had a way for them
to list them as "retired" or whatever, so I could eliminate them from
my search. I think there's a way I can mark to ignore them, but in
this particular instance Team Dakota has moved back to town and
indicated that they may put a couple of them back, so if I mark to
ignore them, then there's no way for me to know if they put the clue
back at some point. That's why I like that AQ has a way for the
PLANTER to update the status of the box. Yes, I'm sure there's a
clutter of missing and retired clues on the AQ database, but since I
never see them, I'm fine with that. I love that I can eliminate
those from my search and only get listings for boxes that have some
likely-hood of being there.

Now the thing that really bugs me is when I find a clue for a box
that lists the last actual find as over a year ago, and the most
recent as all being attempts, but the planter doesn't seem to have
checked it and admitted that it's gone. I wasted a lot of time
looking for obviously missing rest stop boxes a few months ago. Lock
Wench pointed out to me that if you aren't able to check a box right
away because you aren't going to be near it, but a couple of finders
have told you it's missing, you can always list it as "unknown" until
you're able to check for sure. I think this is really responsible on
the part of the planter, and love that the biggest planter is so good
about keeping on top of the status of all her boxes! I think
maintaining over 200 boxes sounds like a nightmare! Go Wenchy!

knitica
