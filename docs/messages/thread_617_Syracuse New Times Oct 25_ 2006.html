Letterboxing is more than a rubber-stamp hobby by Jon Ross

Cynthia read the last clue and rounded the corner. She was in Upper
Onondaga Park in Strathmore, looking for the final letterbox in the
Wonders of Onondaga Park series, the first group of boxes she ever
planted. But instead of searching for the white plastic Wonder Bread
container, she spied the open box strewn over the rock steps. Someone
had found the package and taken the materials--- a homemade stamp and
a small notebook---leaving nothing but the shell of the letterbox.

"Sometimes I'll find a box and it will be chewed up," Cynthia says,
noting that animals uncover materials they come across, but that's
rare. "Almost always it's some person."

Cynthia, who doesn't use her full name but is known to the
letterboxing community as "Lock Wench," picked up the remains of the
box and headed back to the park entrance. This wasn't the first time
this had happened. "When you put a lot of work into a carving, it's
devastating when you find it trashed," she says. "The first one was
devastating to me, but you learn."

Sort of like geocaching, letterboxing began in England and migrated to
the United States primarily by way of an April 1998 article in
Smithsonian Magazine. In the hobby, planters place a homemade rubber
stamp in a box and hide it somewhere---in the woods, among the bricks
of an old building, on the Syracuse University campus. A logbook is
placed inside the box so people who find the package can leave a
"calling card".... their personal stamp on a page. Clues to the box's
location are then posted on the Internet.

Cynthia has already filled seven memo boxes with images of stamps.
She has made some, while she collected others at letterboxing
gatherings, where fellow enthusiasts often trade stamps among
themselves. Internet communities like www.atlasquest.com allow
planters and hunters to publicize new boxes, talk about the hobby and
organize letterboxing events. Atlasquest lists 125 letterboxes in the
Syracuse area.

The most obvious art aspect of letterboxing can be seen as Cynthia
flips through one of her books of stamps, her collection of "miniature
folk art" The images encompass a wide subject matter and even broader
artistic temperament. Some stamps are simple etchings of animals.
Others are amateur in their carving.

A few stand out. One of her favorite stamps is a tight shot focusing
on Tiger Woods' face, a single tear falling down his cheek. Two stamps
in her collection were carved by a fan of director Kevin Smith's movie
Dogma: a replica of the Mooby's sign and a tableau of Bartelby's wingspan.

Emotional and physical investment enters into the carving process---it
takes Cynthia an evening to make a stamp---but artists can't get too
attached to their work. She makes copies of all her images, but even
then she has found it hard to put the finished product in a box and
hide it in the woods. "You have to let go," she says. "It's definately
a temporary art form."

The Internet clues can be just as creative as the stamps. Cynthia's
Wonders series uses flowery language to describe landmarks. Instead of
a gazebo, she writes "shelter on the lake." A bridge becomes the "home
of the troll from the Three Billy Goats Gruff." For a box called "I
Need a Hero," Cynthia embedded the clues in a poem.

Bridget Lichtinger works in the Career Development Center in the S.I.
Newhouse School of Public Communications at SU. While Cynthia devotes
her energy to making letterboxes, Lichtinger mostly looks for them.
"It gets you outside to go hiking," she says. Lichtinger has planted
eight boxes, two on campus. One of her clues asks searchers to think
about where the press "all began." Luchtinger says the best way to
learn about letterboxing is to get together with some friends. read
the rules on the Internet and just do it. At first I didn't quite
understand what it was all about,." she says. "Until you go out to do
it, you're kind of like, huh?"

Losing a letterbox is a hazard, but some older boxers think spreading
the word of the sport contributes to the problem, she says. New
recruits don't know how to play the game and so they ruin it for
everybody. Traditionalists like the clandestine aspect of
letterboxing and were content to pass clues by word of mouth.

"The whole thing is to try to be secretive," Cynthia says. "People
were saying letterboxing was something best shared by a whisper."

She thinks this mistrust of beginners can be avoided if every new
pledge to letterboxing follows a few simple rules. First, be stealthy.
Drawing attention to a letterbox or simply rummaging around with a
notepad and a list of clues can draw attention. Once letterboxers find
a box they should check it out away from the hiding spot and re-hide
the box when people aren't around (Cynthia has whipped out her
cellphone or pretended to take pictures until people leave the area).

"One of the rules, so to speak, is you have to be very inconspicuous,"
Lichtinger says. "You want to be very quiet about it."

Cynthia also cautions people to Leave No Trace and respect the
environment around the box---this means no digging holes to hide
boxes. Finally, letterboxers should re-hide better than the original.
Some people get so excited about finding the box, they forget to hide
it carefully when they are done.

"If people just did those three things, I think the naysayers of
publicity would be fine, " she says. "As it is right now, I just find
it too cool to keep to myself."

From: Bridget Lichtinger | Date: 2006-10-26 13:31:00 UTC
Well, since I have been outed so to speak, I will give the whole story
on what happened.

As everyone in Central York now knows I work for Syracuse University,
S.I. Newhouse School of Public Communications in the Career Development
Center. One of the programs we have is a Master's program in Arts
Journalism and this summer our office was invited to a luncheon to meet
with the master students starting the program. Jon Voss, the reporter
who did the article in the New Times was one of the students at the
luncheon. During the luncheon I was chit chatting about my hobby as a
letterboxer.

Jon must of secured an internship with the New Times and decided to do
story on letterboxing because 1. he had a friend that letterboxed and 2.
he heard about it from someone who worked at SU (me).

On September 11th, yes I remember specifically because my parents were
in town visiting from Ohio, Jon called me on my cell phone. All my
contact information is listed in the staff directory. When he called I
was at the Turning Stone Casino watching my parents playing a mind
numbing amount of slots and watching my inheritance go down the drain
(just kidding!!) and went out to the lobby to talk to Jon. After the
conversation I totally forgot about the article until the discussion
about the Post Standard article - even then I had NO idea he would give
out as much info about me as he did. Why? I don't know because I am
idiot.

Now, I take full responsibility for not saying UP FRONT not to use my
name and contact information. Although it is true he never asked if he
could print it and just did it, I should have known better. Right now I
am kicking myself in the ass...hard. The part that really bothers me is
the printing of the location of my stamp, which is thankfully only
upstairs from me so I can check on frequently and have the permission to
hide, but still.

This has totally changed my mind about talking to press. From now on,
my big ass mouth is shut.

Bridget
