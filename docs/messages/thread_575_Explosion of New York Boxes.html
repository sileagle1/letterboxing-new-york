Explosion of New York Boxes

From: Lock Wench | Date: 2006-09-05 13:48:51 UTC
This week included an explosion of recently planted New York State
boxes. From Buffalo to Albany, from Long Island to Potsdam, and every
point in between; new boxers are dipping their toes in the water and
trying to plant a special box for others to enjoy. And plenty of
experienced boxers have planted one last box before the end of summer!

Please be kind and mindful of all the newbies in this hobby. Many have
read the recent Family Fun article and decided to try this hobby. Some
have been introduced to the hobby by family and friends. Some are
doing a great deal of research first by reading everything they can on
letterboxing.org and atlasquest.com and personal websites while others
are jumping in with very little research at all.

I personally believe it doesn't take that much time to write a
response to a placer and let them know about your experience. If you
like the walk, the visit, the box, the stamp, the clues.....let them
know! If you have issues with a box, still congratulate them on their
effort and give them KIND and GENTLE suggestions on what could make
their boxes even better. I personally am forever indebted to those
more experienced boxers who took the time to write to me, meet me at
events, talk to me online, and basically encourage and gently
instruct me on how I could improve my carving/clue writing/planting
skills.

Letterboxing includes a learning curve and we were all newbies once.
Even some of the most experienced American letterboxers have told me
that they are indebted to those Dartmoor boxers who began this
wonderful hobby. And these same boxers have told me that they are
continually amazed by the creativity, artistic skills, or open and
welcoming spirit of so many new boxers. They also confess that they
have learned new procedures, been introduced to cool locations, have
learned of new supply sources and even some new hiding techniques from
newbies. It's never too late to teach an old dog some new tricks! So
be kind, help educate and support newbies in your area and share some
letterboxing love.

I HAVE to be nicer than you, June....so people will put up with my
mistakes. lol

But seriously...this is a good conversation to have as there are TONS
of new people coming into the hobby and they can use all the help they
can get. I also took the time to read everything I could and yet there
isstill no substitute for going out with an experienced boxer. I
still remember jumping up and down "whooping" in the woods when I
found my first box....one of Catbead's. It was like this weird
confirmation..."wow...it works! You follow the clues exactly and you
will actually find a box!" lol I am sure if she was with me, she would
have arched an eyebrow and whacked me with her fanny pack.

But when I finally had the honor of meeting her and going boxing with
her....I learned SO MUCH! The best part was watching her hide/rehide a
box. This woman is THOROUGH. Not just hidden, but hidden behind a log
or something that will still hide the box from view from the trail if
an animal pulls it out or if a newbie/silverback is in too much of a
hurry to hide properly. And not just covered with some forest litter
but - covered with some forest litter, then a rock to anchor it,( or
under the lip of a ledge or something) and MORE sprinkles of forest
litter to disguise the newly placed rock. And not just looking to see
if you can see anything poking out from the trail, but walking around
it and checking it from all angles. Are you listening, newbies,
parents, scout leaders and class teachers?? :) Let's educate boxers
from the get-go!

When I first started planting, I planted boxes that were available for
everyone as Iwanted to encourage families to do this together. I admit
I gotcarried away (I love to carve, and carved like crazy but then
realizedI would have to plant them all! eek!) but I later started
putting restrictions on some of my boxes. I got a lot of complaints
about it but I don't feel guilty in the least because well over half
of my boxes are open to all. Restrictions I used for more sensitive
areas that required more stealth, or couldn't stand up to a lot of
foot traffic, or just because I wanted to reward people who stuck with
the hobby for more than a week or two.

But I admit I broke the cardinal rule. I planted my first boxes (a
series of 3) before I had found many boxes. So I made ALL the typical
mistakes...notebooks with lined paper, ink in the boxes (they are
liable to freeze or leak in my area and did so before I pulled them
back out), cheaper baggies that didn't close easily so the logbooks
got soaked) and easily compromised hiding spots ( like too close to
kid's playground..where kids would explore every area nearby). I lost
every one of the boxes at some point so I learned to save copies of my
stamps so I could recarve, to use better materials in my boxes, and to
use better camoflage and hiding techniques. But as for that first
series? It's still out there....I'm too sentimental to retire it and
it's close enough to my house that I can check on it regularly. I DID
upgrade the materials, though. :)

Thanks for the warning about using archival ink. I suspect that when
people want to try this hobby they first use what they have at home
before purchasing new stuff which is perfectly understandable. I
know a lot of scrapbookers (and others) use this archival ink and
might decide to start with that.

I've tried wiping it off gently when I find this gunk on a stamp, but
you are right..it doesn't always come off.

So remember, folks....dont use the archival ink! Try a dye based ink (
dries fast but also runs if the logbook gets wet) or a pigment ink
(dries slower but stays put if the logbook gets wet). When using
pigment ink, blot with a slip of paper after stamping in to take up
some of the excess. And in your own logbook, carry a slip of paper
between the pages to help blot so you don't ruin your newly acquired
stamp image.

Yeah, I agree Miss Moon. Yesterday afternoon was so beautiful that after work I took the pups to check on my Historic Auburn boxes. We did not get far (the mosquitos on the trail were HORRIBLE and "someone" forgot the bug spray...whoops) but we made it to the first box, which is hidden in the deep nook of a tree. I came upon it totally uncovered, with only a piece of bark sitting on top (totally exposing all the sides). :o( Upon opening the box and examining the logbook, I saw that the most recent stamped entries were all boxers I have never seen stamped before, or only stamped in a couple logbooks. Newbies. I was furious. It's COMMON SENSE to rehide a box that is hidden--it's what letterboxing is all about. Hidden boxes. You REHIDE them when done. If you don't care to follow the rules, don't play the game. And unfortunately this is not the first time. Mid-summer, checking on some other boxes of mine (most notably Auburn Doubledays), I also found mostly exposed and uncovered, just tossed into it's hiding place. Last person stamped in the boxes was a semi-newbie (I know boxing under a year). I couldn't believe it. It is highly disappointing.

To that end, I have decided I will be pulling my CCC Nature trail boxes permenantly (this is the 9 box "Lucy and Clifford Wildlife Adventures" series). This is a high-use trail behind the college and with all the boxes I've seen left uncovered/sloppily covered lately... I'm afraid they will become trash inadvertantly by some careless boxer who is too excited to get to the next box to care to rehide the one they just found. I am too much of an environmentalist to let this happen. Depending on re-checks of my other boxes in the following months, I will be permanently pulling those as well.

I am very sorry that this is happening. There are concious boxers out there who take the hobby seriously and are responsible boxers. It's a shame when the minority few make it lousy for the majority good, but with the proliferation of new boxers this irresponsibility seems to have gotten worse and worse in the 2 1/2 years I've been boxing. Ethically, I cannot put boxes out into the wild knowing that there are so many people now who will leave them exposed as litter. I am too much of an environmentalist at heart to watch this happen. :o(

I'll probably be pulling the CCC boxes next month (late October most likely) so if you want them, nows your time to stamp them. Depending on my pre-winter checks of my other 20 or so boxes, I will be permanently pulling those as well (if I do, I'll post an email and amend LB.org/Atlasquest so people don't make the trip for nothing!).

Oh my. I am sorry to hear that, Jen. Those are wonderful boxes and I have the pleasure of  having several of your images in my logbooks.   I wouldn't totally blame it on newbies though. I have attended gatherings and have followed RIGHT BEHIND experienced boxers only to watch them barely rehide a box because they were having so much fun chatting with their buddies on the trail!! I have also  followed families who allow children to rehide boxes without supervising and teaching them the proper way to rehide a box.  Thank god there are just as many families that teach and supervise their children; teaching them to walk around the box and look at it from all angles, using an anchor such as a rock or a ledge, and sprinking with plenty of good forest litter/other ground cover. I have even found one of my boxes sitting in the middle of a trail with a couple of tiny bite marks on it ( I barely noticed them) and so I realized a strong little animal had dragged my box out of it's spot.

It is for this reason that I began to majorly camoflage my boxes and provide my own rock if necessary. In the chance that a newbie/untrained child/experienced boxer /animal left the box exposed, at least it now has a fighting chance of lasting until a compassionate and conscientious boxer comes along to rehide it properly.

I also have pulled 17 out of 218 boxes as I have decided that over time certain spots were more likely to be muggled. They just were not good spots ( for whatever reason).  I also use the controversial F-restrictions on some of my boxes. While I've been given a hard time by some boxers I don't feel guilty in the least as the majority of my boxes are open to all. Some boxes are just rewards for those who decide to stay with the hobby.I don't see anything wrong with that. 

I can't stress enough how important EDUCATION is to newbies. I personally benefited from a lot of mentoring from more experienced boxers than myself and I am forever grateful to those who took the time to teach me letterboxing etiquette. Granted, I had done a lot of research on my own but nothing beats boxing with a person more experienced than yourself. I hope you will consider posting on the Newboxers website and re-stress the importance of rehiding well. The moderators announced that almost 60 new people have joined newboxers since the the Family Fun article was published. Now is the chance to make yourself heard. You won't cure very "quick and easy rehider" but you will do the many "eager to learn the right way" consciencious newbies a great service.

art seems to be temporary--but not rehiding makes it needlessly
temporary.

everybody who has recently done one of my boxes:


How do you score on rehiding the box?
Are you a ten? Or do you race from box to box?

1. Scope the area for mugglers before rehiding.
Assign a scout.
2. Deflate, squeeze, seal each and every ziplock with
the zeal of an obsessive compulsive, anally retentive
ferret.
3. Perform a complete contents check to ensure nothing
has been left out of box.
4. Screw, snap, burp the box tightly and
appropriately. Shake & check.
5. Rehouse box in exact location as found. Kneel if
necessary.
6. Replace ALL rocks, leaf litter, branches in an
artful but casual manner.
7. Do a complete 360 sway to make sure the box is not
visible from any horizontal angle.
8. Execute 5 or more stork stretches, peckn bend to
ensure that the box is not visible from any vertical
angle.
9. Rehide the box yourself! Your child, aged
grandmother or newbie friend should NOT do this.
10. Perform final site and perimeter check. Move on
slowly. Speed kills boxes.

I'm a little confused on this one. Is this talk of ink about
deciding which kind is more stamp friendly? One that's neater? One
that's waterproof? I've got ink of all kinds. One could even say
that I'm an ink junkie! Mostly I use the pigment pads, like the
ColorWheel dealies. Pigment is good because it stamps really well,
you can blend colors if you want to, the Petal Points have fine
corners for inking up those small stamp areas, and -I believe- it
holds up to the water test.

I also learned a really neat trick at a stamping convention. If
you're inking up a stamp with more that one pigment inkpad and you
accidentally get some of a darker color onto the pad of a lighter
color, you can just lightly wipe it off with a paper towel. Just
brush the paper towel across the top and it comes right off. Voila!

I always, always, ALWAYS blot off the letterbox stamp after I'm done
with it. If you look at my personal logbooks you'll see that the
back page of each one is my blotter. It looks like one of those
Magic Eye puzzles! I've also gently washed a couple of letterbox
stamps while I was out on the trails. Just poured a little of my
bottled water on it and blotted it with a paper towel.

My $.02 on inkpads!

I've been very happy with my acid free ink pads but I think the real issue here is leaving a stamp as good or better than you found it.
