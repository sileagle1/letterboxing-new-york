Here's more info on the foam:

I use the foam as the backer, alone. It's rigid enough for a backer and if I find an area of my stamp needs extra pressure, the foam is giving enough to let me press that area with a thumb and that usually does it. I mark the area on the foam backer with a sharpie pen if needed and write something like 'press hard here'.

Here's a link to see what the foam looks like as a door hanger,
http://www.stainedglassoriginals.com/kidscraftsupplies_doorhangers.html
It's the first image on the left, and can be found locally in Walmart's crafts section, and crafts stores like Michael's and A.C. Moore. Prices for the 1/4" door hangers run from 3/1.00 on sale to 99¢ each regular price. Bigger sheets about 6mm thick x 9" x 12" are carried by JoAnn Fabric and cost just a dollar or two.  Cut the foam to size with scissors.

The glue I use is "Elmer's Craft Bond 'Ultra Stix All" make sure you get the exact name, Elmer's makes another glue with a similar name. I get it in Walmart in the crafts section and Always squeeze it first before buying to be sure it hasn't hardened in the tube, it has a short shelf life.  Other boxers use a good quality (as in not the cheapest) 5 minute Epoxy.

Lately I've used "Terrifically Tacky Tape" to stick the foam to PZKut, (found in Michaels Crafts near the blank note cards, quilling? strips...close to the stamping stuff like embossing powders & ink pads).  Don't know how it will hold up over Central NY winters, but a sample worked ok in my freezer.  Terifically Tacky Tape is a package of two pages of 6" x 8" double sided super sticky tape.

When I use glue to attach the backer to the PZKut, first I rough up both surfaces with my gouge so that the glue can get a good bite. Then I'm sure to use more glue than I need for a nice thick coat (like icing a cake); spreading with a putty knife on the foam in an area larger than the stamp; press the stamp down firmly/flatly and rotating it a bit to get all the air bubbles out; I also - and this is important - leave a roll of glue around the entire stamp (so it looks like a little bagel or inner-tube), otherwise the glue will shrink when it dries and draw back under the stamp leaving an uneven stamping surface. Lay it on a paper plate and set it aside for a day, then trim up the excess with a blade or scissors.

I use to stamp the image on the back of the foam before gluing, now I prefer to write the stamp name and if there's enough room, my name with a sharpie pen. If you do want to stamp the image on the foam, then "Ancient Page" brand ink won't wash off, but you'll need to heat set it with a heat gun or hair dryer.

Cheers,
catbead
