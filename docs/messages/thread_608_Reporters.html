Ok...here it goes. I met with both the Post Standard reporter and the
reporter from the Syracuse New Times. I insisted that both of them
accompany me on a letterboxing outing and both did so willingly. They
stamped in and everything, using a store bought stamp. I only brought
them to my own boxes and they both seemed to really enjoy it. The New
Times reporter and I did one of my 3 box series, where we discovered
the second box peeking out of it's hiding spot and the third box
scattered all over the ground..both the book and the stamp missing. It
sounds weird, but I was actually glad it happened as it was a vivid
example of what we planters face all the time. A quick perusal of the
logbook confirmed that the last finder was a new family who had just
discovered letterboxing in a Family Fun article and had rushed out to
make their first finds. I wrote them a gentle letter telling them
about the trashed box and reminded them about rehiding boxes better
than they find them and suggested they take the time to do some more
research on the various sites.

With both reporters I tried to stress the only real rules I know for
letterboxing, besides general boxing etiquette.
'
1) Be stealthy.
2) Rehide boxes better than you found them.
3) Leave no trace.

I spoke with both reporters about the publicity issue and our general
policy of sharing public spaces with geocachers. Both reporters seemed
to be sensitive to the publicity controversy and both agreed it was a
wonderful hobby for people of all ages and abilities..with something
for everyone. Both said they wanted to continue and become active
letterboxers.

Both reporters said they contacted multiple people, and said they were
directed by their editors to keep contacting boxers until they found
someone willing to talk with them...newbies and oldtimers alike.
Neither wanted to do an article without letterboxer input, which I
really appreciated.

I spent an average of 4 hours with each reporter. I hope local boxers
will feel I made some effort to educate the public and the media about
our hobby in a responsible and sensitive way. If they don't like the
outcome, then I hope they will make an effort to contribute in the future.

From: Lock Wench | Date: 2006-10-15 03:40:38 UTC
I have no idea when the stories will hit the papers, although I let each of the reporters know that the other was working on the same project. Both of them guessed probably the first week of November. I will definately try to post them here so people can judge and take potshots at me. :)  But seriously, with so many of my boxes planted in the area, I have the most to lose if the publicity hurts the hobby. I spoke to the reporters so I could have some measure of control regarding what got discussed.

If you are a new boxer/family that is really making a conscious effort to rehide boxers better than you find them, then you will be a very popular addition to this hobby. I know some boxers who take the stamp image without stamping in, as they don't count their finds anyway...so you can never really tell for sure who was the last visitor. But you can get a pretty good idea.  Boxes get uncovered for all kinds of reasons, but every planter will appreciate you taking the time to cover them well. It's not necessary to let the planter know you re-covered something, as it is expected you will do a decent job. But any kind of damage or missing parts should definately be reported to the planter. Fix something if you can, but let the planter know so they can check on it when they are in the area.

Letterboxing is spreading exponentially, and has been for several years. Think of it as a popular chain letter. Once it is out there it gets spread on and on and on. Over 100 articles have been written in just the last couple of years, as it is hard to ignore a hobby which has such positive aspects and can be shared by so many different kinds of people. These two reporters each had heard of letterboxing in different ways. One was told by her editor, and the other had heard about it in a meeting they shared with a local boxer. Both said they found local boxers by searching letterboxing.org and atlasquest.com and writing to a selection of  them.

As for the newbie designation? I am still called a newbie even though I have over 300 finds and 200 plants. Doesn't bother me in the least. I like to consider myself "newbie-friendly" anyhow.  Remember, EVERY boxer you meet was a newbie once. Some are just friendlier than others.

from home etc). I am trying to separate letterboxing from geocaching
and show the reasons why people choose one over the other.

Regarding beginning boxers, I think you are being unreasonably harsh.
After I had found about 20 boxes, I was lucky enough to go boxing with
a seasoned boxer who let me hide the rehide the box under her
supervision. She showed me how even my very thick covering of leaves
and other forest debris could be blown off by a heavy wind or dragged
off by an animal. She showed me why my carefully arranged pile of
sticks (while covering the box completely) screamed: HERE IS A
LETTERBOX!!!!!!! She showed me why a scattering of leaves, PLUS a rock
for weight and then MORE forest litter gave a box a fighting chance.
How hiding on the far side of the log still hid the box from passersby
if the covering somehow was compromised. How a box wedged under a rock
edge can work as well as a loose rock to prevent an animal from
dragging it off. How retracing your steps into an area lessens the
chance of a "spur trail" being formed directly to the box. I took her
lessons even farther and now spray paint all my Lock n Locks with
plastic Fusion spraypaint and cover the flat areas with camo tape. I
don't plant next to playgrounds (except for one notable exception) or
anywhere else kids are likely to explore and discover a box.

The reason I am gentle the first time is because this seasoned boxer
was gentle with me. She saw that I had good intent, but little
experience. She knew I wasn't trying to rush and she knew I wasn't
trying to "disrespect the boxer." She didn't just YELL at me to
rehide better, she TAUGHT me how to rehide better. She didn't bash me
on the boards and treat me like a clueless idiot, but as someone who
could learn very quickly if just given 10-15 minutes of her valuable
time. It is for this reason that I will continue to camo my boxes,
teach when I can, and have my FIRST letter to a boxer be a gentle one
and not make assumptions.
