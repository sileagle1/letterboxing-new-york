From: catbead1 | Date: 2005-02-28 00:08:27 UTC
Our all time best decoy was with the dog and trying to replace a box
under the noses of a group of people who just would not leave. It was
getting late, we were tired and still had to drive home. Our dog can
be quite the attention grabber when we get her jumping for sticks and
racing after a ball, so we had her perform. Fly Doc had the box
tucked in his jacket, and when the group of people went back to their
conversation I *accidently* tossed the ball into the general hiding
area and Fly Doc just happened to have *trouble* picking it up. Box
replaced and we left chuckling to ourselves that not one of the group
knew what happened.

Another thing we do is Fly Doc acts as a lookout and gives a heads-up
whistle. If there's time I'll go back to him, otherwise I'll grab the
camera and act like I'm taking pictures muttering things like "oh,
look at that", "isn't that beautiful", "look at that rock", etc. So
far everyone's walked on by.

Once I was without camera and someone walked by and asked what I was
doing (I was planting a box and had just enough time to turn & take a
step away from it), the only thing I could think to say was that I was
admiring the ferns. It seemed to satisfy them.

Cheers,

From: Paul Gonyea | Date: 2005-02-28 02:37:12 UTC
I've been known to suddenly turn into a botanist who specializes in studying the tiny plants that grow around logs, rocks, and letterboxes. If there are other people around, I'll study nearby ferns and mosses, taking my time as I approach that elusive and fascinating specimen, letterboxus concealedis. 
 
After watching me for a short while, people get accustomed to what I'm doing, and lose interest. That's when I discretely grab the letterbox and act as if I had it in my daypack all along. I'll pull out my journal and pretend to be intensely focused on taking notes. Sometimes it appears to strangers that I'm making nature prints -- but anyone watching would see that I always leave things right where I found them.
 
This scheme has worked really well -- but the key is not to rush -- the observers have to get used to you poking around first.
 
Paul in SF
