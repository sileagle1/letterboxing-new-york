Oh what a tangled web. I am growing weary over the publicity issue.
Why does every boxer think this was going to be a secret forever and
ever?? And alongside an even more popular hobby called geocaching???
If you want to be pissed at publicity, then write to the Smithsonian
and chastise them for bringing it to the USA. Why not write hate mail
to Tom Cooch and Rae Record and Randy Hall, etc and complain that they
have ruined the hobby by sharing it and writing a how-to book? SEE HOW
RIDICULOUS THIS SOUNDS?? Recent articles in GAMES magazine, Family
Fun and other publications have opened this hobby to the masses. Choi,
LBNA's webmaster, just did a television interview on Chronicle in New
England. Well, God bless as I never would have found this hobby
without Smithsonian Magazine.

That being said, people are understandably upset that boxes might come
up missing when overly enthusiastic newbies charge into the woods and
streets to find boxes. Well well..I was one of those overly
enthusiastic newbies as well. And I didn't screw up a single box.

Look, people. I have lost many boxes in the past.MANY. It's sad and
upsetting but then I had to become more ZEN about my boxes and do what
I could to protect them. I try to tell myself that they are temporary
art anyway, not under lock and key and alarm systems in some museum.
It's still upsetting to see all that hard work be lost so I have also
learned to camoflage them better, put F restrictions on boxes that
might require more stealth or are in sensitive areas, make some
special boxes WOM and spread EDUCATION wherever and however I can.
Yes, I was hired to teach a class (as a fundraiser) and I do even
more for nothing...donating my time and even materials. Yes, I've
spoken to students at Syracuse University and hikers in the
Adrirondacks. I've only shown my OWN boxes and my OWN clues. I teach
people how to do the proper research to find other boxes after they've
tested the waters and decided if it's something they want to stay
with. THE CAT HAS LONG BEEN OUT OF THE BAG AND THE SHIP HAS SAILED. So
let's do what we can to protect our boxes and make sure people learn
the same way WE DID. Through patient instruction, kind encouragement
and a loving and open manner.

Let the stoning commence.

From: Tracy Baxter Haynes | Date: 2006-09-22 12:29:16 UTC
Ooo, I hope there will be *no* stoning :-)  After all, this is our little friendly state board, not the *big* list.  LOL 
 
I think it is no secret that I am not a fan of the mass, overt publicity of our hobby.  I like the idea of a more gradual, organic growth of letterboxing.  This is a *special* hobby, it is unique.  I like the idea of its existence being passed around from one trusted friend to another.  I learned of this hobby from a friend, who learned from a friend, etc.  I felt honored that they wanted to share with me the secrets that were lying out there, waiting to be found.  They took me out, searched for boxes with me and showed me how to stamp my thumbprint in - I didn't even have a stamp yet!  Once I was hooked, they showed me how to obtain supplies, design and carve a signature stamp. 
 
There are some who will say that they never would have found this hobby if it wasn't for some type of mass publicity, be it an article, an interview or a lecture.  I don't mean to sound harsh but such is life.  No one is entitled to know about or be exposed to everything.  If I had not been told of this hobby, my life would have still gone on as usual.  I would have been out hiking with my dogs and friends anyways.  I would have gotten to know a few less people, seen a few less different places, but, oh well.  I am sure there are a lot more hobbies and things out there, that if I knew about them, I would enjoy them.  What we don't know about, we can't miss. 
 
While some might be tired of batting around the same old topics, I think reasonable and measured discussion of these is valuable, especially to the new people finding out about the hobby.  I like knowing what other people think about publicity.  It helps me to clarify my thoughts.  Hey, it might even change my mind (hasn't happened yet in this case, but I am always open to being convinced I am wrong LOL).  LW is right in that we do need to protect our own boxes and we do need to educate and instruct when we can and the opportunities arise.
 
~artichoke

From: Lock Wench | Date: 2006-09-22 15:07:54 UTC
Yes, no stoning here! I got into many heated discussions when I visited CT this past weeknd and it was very interesting seeing how points of view differ from state to state. NY is still in it's relative infancy so we are unaffected by some of what goes on in the more letterbox-populated states.

I do not seek out publicity. I do not contact newspapers or  magazine writers or reporters or offer up classes to groups. . But if I am contacted by an individual or group, or if I know an article is being written and the reporter is going into with NO input from anyone in the letterboxing community, you can bet your bottom dollar that I am going to contact them.  I try to make sure an article has a fighting chance of being semi-accurate. As I said before, anyone holding on to the dream of this hobby going back to the old days is deceiving themselves.

Our Catbead is often contacted by the local media as she has a personal website that is sub-labeled as Boxing in Syracuse, and her link is posted on many lists and websites.  Any reasonable internet search will bring up her contact information. However, she chooses not to speak to the press and I respect that. While she would be a perfect candidate, as she has a long history of local boxing and has an excellent educational style, I can understand why she shys away from speaking up. No worries. I did contact that reporter she listed, as many of you expected I would. I explained to the reporter how many in the letterboxing community would prefer a more "friend -to -friend" organic growth to the hobby and he said he had heard much of that angle from the "finders" he had spoken to.  I was pleased when he told me that he was going letterboxing with some letterboxing friends so that he could learn first-hand about this hobby before he wrote anything.  That is doing more than most writers do, and l commend him for that.

I learned of this hobby from my mother, who sent me the original Smithsonian article, which I still have. I was a late starter but I have always been grateful that she shared this info with me.  I finally  found a hobby which combines all my loves.

LW

From: Jen Z | Date: 2006-09-22 19:23:25 UTC
I sat thinking about this email for a few hours and I've decided I'm going to throw in my 2 cents, worth 1 after all the NYS taxes are taken out. :oP And I probably won't be able to say it as articulately as my bud Artichoke, either.

I am also growing weary over the publicity issue, for the exact opposite reason I suppose. :oP I guess I am one of those stodgy old-fashioned boxers who prefer the hobby being secretive, stealthy, and "grown organically", as Artichoke wrote. I *do* hate hearing about all the publicity LB'ing gets in every-other-magazine nowadays, I *do* cringe when I see huge groups of people --kids or adults-- sprawled all over the trail with the box strewn here and there (as I came across in Jamesville last fall with some 20+ Brownie/Girl Scout troop clomping through the sensitive underbrush after a box -- so nice. This really turned my opinion that boxing should not be a hobby done in huge groups, for the sake of stealth, the box's protection, and for the environment's sake.). I ALSO hate it when boxes go missing, whether it be from punks, animals, kids, young folks, old folks, whatever- it's a small piece of artwork I spent a lot of time working on and I am miserable when they go missing-! Crucify me for it. I can't help it... I love doing artwork, I love carving, and when a box goes missing it means it became litter which is totally against the environmentalist in me. I can't get "zen" about my art becoming litter no matter how hard I try. :o(

That doesn't make me -or anyone else who believes in the more traditional aspects of LB'ing-- anti-newbie, anti-education, anti-Girl Scouts, anti- magazines, or anti- anything else. I suppose it makes us traditionalists (and I'm not surprised I fall to that side of the fence, because I still contentedly listen to my records/LP's and cassettes, while most of the world bounds away on IPods, M3P's, DVD's and other newfangled gadgets). :o)

The Cat may be out of the Bag with all this raring publicity --and it IS-- but personally I think it would be best to put up some sort of fence around the yard so the cat doesn't run out into the highway and get hit by a truck. IMHO, anyway.
