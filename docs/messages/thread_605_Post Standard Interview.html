That opens one of the hot debates here (and on other lists). They are
generally two camps. One general group would prefer letterboxing
remain traditionally more secret and be an activity spread by word of
mouth and so they shun any requests for interviews from any media
outlet. There is another general group that feels the hobby has
already been "outed" and so speaks to the press in order to educate
new people entering the hobby so that common problems can hopefully be
avoided.

Prior to the Wonders event, some enthusiastic young boxers spoke to a
local newspaper and discussed the upcoming Wonders event as part of
their interview. This caused the local state park manager at the
hosting site to "panic" about potentially large crowds and so he
instituted last minute rules (days before the event!!) that used
Geocaching rules. Geocaching rules included permits, limitations on
number of boxes (15), limitations on hiding spots (only 10 foot off of
trails), minimum distances of 1/4 mile between boxes, as well as
upping their event fee. Only quick thinking by the event planners
avoided a disaster. I am placing NO BLAME on the boxers who were
interviewed, but only bring this up to show that education is NEEDED.
Not just for the new boxers, but for park personnel, the media and the
general public.

We can continue to let these educational opportunities pass by but I
personally think letterboxing is already OUT there (Silent Doug's
letterboxing.info site tracks news articles/interviews about
letterboxing and his site lists 100 already but I know there are
dozens more that aren't listed). A Time magazine article brought in a
great many of new boxers a couple of years ago...including many
"veterans" you read about on various sites and lists. Even Mark Pepe
didn't start boxing until mid 2002 and that was after reading an
article in the Hartford Courant that mentioned letterboxing. I think
many would agree that his column has been a nice addition to the
letterboxing world and yet he and others never would have found
letterboxing without these news stories. Home School magazines have
been publishing articles for YEARS about letterboxing..and the home
schoolers are some of the most active boxers out there.

I agree that WOM is the preferred way to spread letterboxing. But I
also believe those days are long gone and now just hope to mitigate
problems by encouraging responsible interviews and urge interviewees
to ask for proof reading privileges.

Perhaps this group could come up with a list of topics/points to be
made in media interviews?

LW

*My apologies if I misspoke for the traditional/secretive camp. Please
feel free to clarify/expand upon your position on media interviews*

Agreed. Blue diamonds, publicity, extreme boxes.....they are all welcomed to be discussed on this forum, rather than debated.
And as for the skittish park manager, it's because he has obviously dealt with large crowds for geocaching events. There are big misconceptions about letterboxing when it is compared to geocaching. From my recent talks with a few state park managers as well as my previous experience as a geocacher...I can tell you that the biggest reasons permits were instituted is because of damage to local fauna and placement of "extreme" caches.  GPS units are accurate up to about 20 feet although some caches are EXACTLY on the coordinates if you can get a strong enough signal (gps units depend on  satellite signals so a heavy tree canopy or extreme cloud cover can affect the signal). That last 20 feet you have to rely on your "geocaching vision"  ....much like our letterboxing vision. But newbie geocachers often trample an area while sweeping over that last 20 foot diameter..while letterboxes tend to take you RIGHT TO THE BOX.
Extreme letterboxes ( as determined by AQ's definition) are those which require special skills or equipment in order to retrieve the box, and you are told that up front.  I have gone after numerous caches only to find at the last moment that the cache has been placed over the lip of a cliff or along a knife edge. A trinket is not worth the danger, in my own opinion.

So my bottom line is, I guess I am sick and tired of being forced to play by geocaching rules because of a lack of understanding/education about letterboxing.
