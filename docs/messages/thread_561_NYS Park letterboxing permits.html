From: Walter Quinn III | Date: 2006-08-02 14:06:26 UTC
As the one who probably helped prompt this conversation with a trip to Letchworth this weekend, I'll jump in.
 
As for what you said, LW, about bringing boxers to lesser known parts of areas, little gems, etc. I agree, and think it's a lovely idea, and do enjoy it when others do that for me with their boxes. However, it does have a downside (at least, for me).
Let me give you an example using this weekend- I travelled to Letchworth with my family for a camping weekend. Once there, we didnt' leave the park. So, I ended up doing NO letterboxing (other than scouting locations int he park for potential boxes), because there were no boxes in the park for me to find.
For me, it's hard to just get up and wander around towns looking for letterboxes 'near' where I am- in particular, when i'm on vacation. This is because I don't have a vehicle to use. So, I appreciate when there's boxes in the immediate vicinity of where I am- such as a grand, open, and just totally HUGE park like Letchworth.
 
My feeling about letterboxing has always been along the lines of 'oh, neat! There's a letterbox THERE? Cool!' When I first got into letterboxing nearly two years ago, I was amazed at all the places that had letterboxes already- not only a few places that I frequently visted or at least passed by, but also placed I wanted to go, as well. I went to Letchworth four years ago, long before I knew about letterboxing. When I found out there were a few in the area, and even one that I thought was in the park, I was excited about this coming trip.
 
Now, that moves me onto the topic of the thread- the NYS park permit thing.
I was at Letchworth, and I gotta say, it could easily be a letterboxing haven. I saw so many great places to hide boxes, and many of them I've already begun contimplating the designs for, because approximately half of them will be site specific.
 
Then I came home and hopped on AQ, and after drudging through the insane amount of messages and mail I had, was reminded of this disappointing permit issue.
However- I am optimistic.
I would be more than willing to cooperate with the park on planting letterboxes, even to the point of getting a permit, under the condition that it wouldn't be costly, like I know it is in some areas down south.
There are places where to plant one letterbox in a state park, you need to purchase a permit for $30. This permit lasts one year, and then must be renewed (with another $30. And then, the permit only covers one letterbox- which means if you want to plant more, you have to get ADDITIONAL permits.
I'm sorry, but I just do not have tha tkind of money. I already struggle sometimes to find money for the SUPPLIES to make these letterboxes. I'm a poor college student! I had to hitch a ride to Syracuse just to attend a letterboxing event!
 
However, I do admit that I do not know exactly the full policy regarding the NYS Park permits- I'll be finding out soon, I hope. But I can not afford to cooperate if it involves the kind of money and registration that parks down south require.
If the permits WERE to cost money, I would consider it as long as it was a one-time fee, and covered multiple box planting. I will not get permits for multiple boxes, and I won't be renewing a permit, either (at least, not until I have a stable way to pay that!).
IT is my hope that the permits will be easy to obtain, and will not cost money that I dont' have. In addition, if all the park employees are asking is to be told and worked with when planting letterboxes, then that is easy enough- I know they don't want to lead people into dangerous areas, and neither do I.
 
So, I'll probably have more on the subject once I find out exactly what this permit deal entails.
 
CQ (stepping away from the computer to breathe...as well as put on pants, cause he's finally gotten out of bed)

Not sure if any of you saw the following post from Ryan (Atlasquest owner, webmaster and moderator)  on the Atlasquest New York board, but here it is, along with a personal note I received from Ryan and my posted response. Can we have a discussion about this? I would love to hear points of view from others and how they handled their similar  situation. Did you encounter nice Park personnel willing to work with you? Did you design learning  about the location into your stamp/box?  Did you just pull the box or did you ask for a permit? Let's hear from you all out there!

Post from Ryan on Atlasquest: Hello, New Yorkers! =) I got this e-mail a few minutes ago from a guy in the New York SP system:

"You should be advised that permission is needed via permit from New York State Park Managers in order to place a letter box in a state park. There are rules for geocaches that also apply to letterboxes. (state park management has set spefic rules for placement of such things) Most park managers will work with you in placement of the letterboxes when done so through the park manager. When letterboxes are found without the proper permit they will most likely be removed and the owner notified."

I'm not sure if he's refering to a specific box or not, but let it be known, permits are needed if you want to plant a box in a New York SP and they DO know about Atlas Quest so don't think you can sneak around them by posting here instead of LbNA. ;o)


Mail from Ryan:

I wasn't sure if the ranger from the New York State Parks was e-mailing me about a specific box or not and asked, and this was the reply:

"Ryan,
I apologize for not being specific. There was a box put in the Minna Anthony
Common Nature Center called something like Girls Night Out, I found it, removed itand emailed the owner. Getting permission to put the box in a park is quite easy
the person just has to ask and follow the rules! Thanks for getting back to me.

Randy "

Is that you? =) I can't find a box called "Girls Night Out" on AQ, but I swear I've heard it before and I swear it sounds like something you would do. ;o)

-- Ryan

My response on AQ

Ryan -

Thanks for posting about this. I am not that thrilled about planting in state parks anyway, as I try to bring people to those lesser known places that they might not have seen before. Most of my 16 (out of 215!) state park boxes are in lesser known and visited state parks. But having worked with NY State Parks personnel before (I used to work for a prominent architecture/ landscape architecture firm here in NY), I tried to anticipate their concerns and so planted "location" based stamps. You know what I mean... the stamp was about the place and I gave a lot of background about the area and sang it's praises....trying to educate the public. I enjoy finding location stamps myself (so looking at my logbook is sort of like a roadmap of everywhere I've been!). I also love learning about new places and I find that searchers of my boxes are appreciative of that sort of thing as well. In the beginning, I just carved anything that struck my fancy, but now I carve mostly location boxes with perhaps the occasional nearby fun "bonus" box thrown in.

The few park personnel that HAVE contacted me have been really cool and happy to let me leave my boxes as is. Only one had my box moved slightly, as they were doing some trail work. When I plant, I make sure I avoid nesting areas and sensitive plants such as fern beds and the like. It's easy to pick up a brochure about the area BEFORE you plant, learn something about the place and then avoid the sensitive spots. I avoid animal holes in trees, placing boxes in ancient rock walls etc. I anticipate the path that might form to the box and so make that path over rocks or pine needles or similar non-sensitive ground cover. I learned from Catbead that under a bridge is a good hiding place for just this reason! All those long hikes with my parents while my mother droned on about plants and their latin names (blah blah blah) must have sunk in a little! lol

After I read your post, I went back through my list and was pleasantly surprised that my 16 state park boxes are in 10 parks. I think I will take the tactic of contacting each of the park personnel that I haven't spoken to already and let them know about my box, invite them to find it by providing them the clues, and asking them to contact me to pick up the box if they feel it is in an inappropriate area or needs a permit. I've introduced more than a couple of park personnel to letterboxing this way and they seem to enjoy the fact that I lead them RIGHT TO THE BOX, as the majority of my boxes are hidden with families in mind. I encourage all New Yorkers to give letterboxers a good reputation as land and nature stewards by doing the same.

LW

Although I love the secretive nature of this hobby, I do think that New York State Parks have done a good job of working WITH letterboxers, instead of banning the hobby as other states have done.  Please share your own experiences.

I only mention the Wellesley IS thing (welcome to
geocachers) as curious in that the thread seems to
indicate that NY parks are possibly not very welcoming
to this type of activity in general. If they are OK
with caches then it would seem letterboxes should be
okay too. Or perhaps some PR is needed to explain the
difference between the 2 activities. As far a
letterboxers being more rogue-ish (sp?) there is one
park here near Kingston that has removed all the
caches but allowed the letterboxes to stay! The park
superintendent there sees the 2 hobbies as being very
different and hopes to create 2 distinct policies.
The caches were too far off the trail, too large and
the directions to these caches were so vague that
cachers were ripping up the environs looking for them.
He sees letterboxing as a more family type of
activity and one that can enhance the park experience
esp when box themes reflect park features. That's
what we need to promote! Nor

From: Lock Wench | Date: 2006-08-03 00:52:25 UTC
CQ-- There is no fee for these permits. You just have to fill out an application, get a sticker and place in on your cache/letterbox. As part of the application, you have to agree to maintain/visit your cache/letterbox bi-annually. Read over the application, contact the park manager in your region, explain letterboxing if necessary, and take it from there.
 
Good luck!

When I found out there were a few in the area, and even one that I thought was in the park, I was excited about this coming trip.
 
Now, that moves me onto the topic of the thread- the NYS park permit thing.
I was at Letchworth, and I gotta say, it could easily be a letterboxing haven. I saw so many great places to hide boxes, and many of them I've already begun contimplating the designs for, because approximately half of them will be site specific.
 
Then I came home and hopped on AQ, and after drudging through the insane amount of messages and mail I had, was reminded of this disappointing permit issue.
However- I am optimistic.
I would be more than willing to cooperate with the park on planting letterboxes, even to the point of getting a permit, under the condition that it wouldn't be costly, like I know it is in some areas down south.
There are places where to plant one letterbox in a state park, you need to purchase a permit for $30. This permit lasts one year, and then must be renewed (with another $30. And then, the permit only covers one letterbox- which means if you want to plant more, you have to get ADDITIONAL permits.
I'm sorry, but I just do not have tha tkind of money. I already struggle sometimes to find money for the SUPPLIES to make these letterboxes. I'm a poor college student! I had to hitch a ride to Syracuse just to attend a letterboxing event!
 
However, I do admit that I do not know exactly the full policy regarding the NYS Park permits- I'll be finding out soon, I hope. But I can not afford to cooperate if it involves the kind of money and registration that parks down south require.
If the permits WERE to cost money, I would consider it as long as it was a one-time fee, and covered multiple box planting. I will not get permits for multiple boxes, and I won't be renewing a permit, either (at least, not until I have a stable way to pay that!).
IT is my hope that the permits will be easy to obtain, and will not cost money that I dont' have. In addition, if all the park employees are asking is to be told and worked with when planting letterboxes, then that is easy enough- I know they don't want to lead people into dangerous areas, and neither do I.
 
So, I'll probably have more on the subject once I find out exactly what this permit deal entails.
 
CQ (stepping away from the computer to breathe...as well as put on pants, cause he's finally gotten out of bed)

Ryan -
Thanks for posting about this. I am not that thrilled about planting in state parks anyway, as I try to bring people to those lesser known places that they might not have seen before. Most of my 16 (out of 215!) state park boxes are in lesser known and visited state parks. But having worked with NY State Parks personnel before (I used to work for a prominent architecture/ landscape architecture firm here in NY), I tried to anticipate their concerns and so planted "location" based stamps. You know what I mean... the stamp was about the place and I gave a lot of background about the area and sang it's praises....trying to educate the public. I enjoy finding location stamps myself (so looking at my logbook is sort of like a roadmap of everywhere I've been!). I also love learning about new places and I find that searchers of my boxes are appreciative of that sort of thing as well. In the beginning, I just carved anything that struck my fancy, but now I carve mostly location boxes with perhaps the occasional nearby fun "bonus" box thrown in.
The few park personnel that HAVE contacted me have been really cool and happy to let me leave my boxes as is. Only one had my box moved slightly, as they were doing some trail work. When I plant, I make sure I avoid nesting areas and sensitive plants such as fern beds and the like. It's easy to pick up a brochure about the area BEFORE you plant, learn something about the place and then avoid the sensitive spots. I avoid animal holes in trees, placing boxes in ancient rock walls etc. I anticipate the path that might form to the box and so make that path over rocks or pine needles or similar non-sensitive ground cover. I learned from Catbead that under a bridge is a good hiding place for just this reason! All those long hikes with my parents while my mother droned on about plants and their latin names (blah blah blah) must have sunk in a little! lol
After I read your post, I went back through my list and was pleasantly surprised that my 16 state park boxes are in 10 parks. I think I will take the tactic of contacting each of the park personnel that I haven't spoken to already and let them know about my box, invite them to find it by providing them the clues, and asking them to contact me to pick up the box if they feel it is in an inappropriate area or needs a permit. I've introduced more than a couple of park personnel to letterboxing this way and they seem to enjoy the fact that I lead them RIGHT TO THE BOX, as the majority of my boxes are hidden with families in mind. I encourage all New Yorkers to give letterboxers a good reputation as land and nature stewards by doing the same.
LW
Although I love the secretive nature of this hobby, I do think that New York State Parks have done a good job of working WITH letterboxers, instead of banning the hobby as other states have done.  Please share your own experiences.

CQ-- There is no fee for these permits. You just have to fill out an application, get a sticker and place in on your cache/letterbox. As part of the application, you have to agree to maintain/visit your cache/letterbox bi-annually. Read over the application, contact the park manager in your region, explain letterboxing if necessary, and take it from there.
 
Good luck!
