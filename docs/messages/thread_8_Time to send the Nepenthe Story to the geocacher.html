Since I belong to 3 Yahoo! Letterboxing Groups: LbNA (the national
group), LbCNA (Northern California), and LbCNY (Central NY), it's
easy to forget which one you're posting messages to...

In this case, I may have shared this info with Central NY simply to
inform them about geocaching. If you're not familiar with it, it's
somewhat like letterboxing, but uses GPS (Global Positioning Systems)
to give coordinates of "caches". Generally, there aren't puzzles to
solve, you put the coordinates in on your GPS system and go to that
spot. I believe they are accurate to within 10 feet. Their caches
don't have rubber stamps. Instead, there are small trinkets inside,
and you take one, and you leave one in its place.

The word, Nepenthe, comes from Greek mythology. It was a potion that
caused forgetfulness of all troubles, worries, and sorrows. (Don't
you wish there was such a potion?)

My letterbox, Nepenthe,
http://home.earthlink.net/~pgonyea/nepenthe.htm
a favorite of many people in this area, was in a beautiful, peaceful,
and relaxing location, suffered badly by being too close to a
geocache. In fact, it was in almost the exact same spot, but about 4
feet below the geocache. What happened was that a geocacher found
Nepenthe, and thinking it was a cache, took the rubber stamp, and
left a dumb bottle cap in its place. (Letterboxers call
this "geotrashing).

Later, someone else also made the mistake, but then found the cache,
too, and put Nepenthe up in the same niche as the cache. Then more
geocachers wrote all sorts of dumb things in the journal, and to
letterboxers, it looked like Nepenthe was missing. I was left with a
bottle cap, and a journal with remarks in it such as, "You will get
laid tonight". The letterboxing community, as I know it, is much more
considerate and thoughtful than these folks were. It was my first
encounter with a geocache, and not a good one.

When I found Nepenthe with the geocache, I looked up the planter on
wwww.geocaching.org, and wrote them a polite e-mail (even though I
was fuming mad), asking if we could work out something so that this
wouldn't happen again. They never responded back. If I were a mean
and nasty person (believe me, I'm tempted), I would remove their
cache.

For people who had found Nepenthe and e-mailed me, I offered to send
them a piece of beautiful music (MP3), entitled Nepenthe, that
inspired the box, and related it to a personal story about my visit
to a place in the Greek Isles 16 years ago in 1988: Lindos on the
Island of Rhodes. There, the remains of a ancient greek temple to
Helios, the sun god, stands at the top of a 400 foot cliff
overlooking the deep blue Aegean Sea. At that time, I had just been
diagnosed with a very fatal disease, and didn't expect to live past
one year. While I stood there, depressed, I thought to myself that I
would never return to that spot again.

Well, you see I'm still alive and healthy. And I returned to Lindos
in 2001. In my living room are 2 photos taken of me at the exact same
spot -- miraculously. They serve to remind me that I need to always
reserve some hope for the possibility of the impossible happening.

THAT'S what I wanted the geocacher to realize that my letterbox,
Nepenthe, stood for. If you read towards the end of my clues for
Nepenthe, you'll see that it stands for the blessings of family,
friends, and life.

It's a long story, but an important one.

I'd be happy to send anyone in this group the music as well. It's a
large file (2.5 MB), so Hotmail accounts, for example, won't accept
it. Most people can receive it without a problem. E-mail me at
pgonyea@... if you'd like to hear it.


Paul in SF

From: catbead1 | Date: 2004-09-29 21:23:47 UTC
Paul, I'm happy to see you with us.

I'm aware of one case of a stamp being taken by a geocacher, and with
persistence it was returned to its rightful owner. I think the owner
not only emailed the person who took the stamp, but went on the
geocache website and posted message(s) asking for its return. By now,
your stamp may have been traded & left in another cache. Maybe by
posting a message it will be seen by the person who has it, or has
seen it, and they will contact you. It also wouldn't hurt to expand
their knowledge base. Who knows, next time someone will recognise a
letterbox and leave it alone.

Not all geocachers are tramplers and destroyers, some are careful,
considerate folks who also letterbox....although to anyone who's had
their box hijacked or destroyed that's probably not very comforting.
I'd be pretty angry myself if it happened to me.

I hope you get it back.
Take care,
catbead
